Annotation Guidelines for Paraphrase Alignment

Introduction

These guidelines are based on the annotation guidelines for the Edinburgh++ corpus by Chris Callison-Burch, Trevor Cohn, and Mirella Lapata. In this task, you will be presented with pairs of paraphrases. These paraphrases may be of varying quality, but are intended to convey the same meaning with different words. Aligning sentence pairs

We do not have a strict definition of what constitutes a correspondence. As a rule of thumb align words or phrases \(a \leftrightarrow b\) in two sentences \((A, B)\) whenever the words a can be substituted for \(b\) in \(B\), or vice-versa. This relationship should hold within the context of the sentence pair in question: the relation \(a \leftrightarrow b\) need not hold in general contexts. Trivially this definition allows for identical word pairs.

The choice of aligning words or blocks can be subjective. In general, you should align the smallest possible sensible unit, which will be individual words in the majority of sentences. When the same concept has been realised in the two sentences with quite a different construction, and aligning the two word-for-word becomes infeasible, then a many-to-many block should be used. carbon dioxide emissions block aligns to greenhouse gasses but there is no correspondence between any one of the words in the sentences. In general, discontinuous alignments should be preferred over many-to-many alignments.

Special Cases

This section describes some general heuristics to decide how to make alignments in cases of ambiguity. The rules described here are fairly general and are simple to remember and rely on.

Named Entities

Named entities denote people, places, organisations, etc. They are often expressed by proper noun phrases (NPs) (e.g., Barack Obama, the United Nations) but can also be attested as definite descriptions (e.g., the prime minister, the president of the organisation). When two sentences have identical named entities, then these should be aligned on a word-by-word basis. For example for the pair Barack Obama won and Barack Obama was victorious you should produce the following alignments: Barack ↔ Barack, Obama ↔ Obama and won ↔ was victorious.

When a sentence pair has two named entites that refer to the same individual, organisation, or location but differ in surface form, then you should use block alignments. So, Obama should be block aligned with Barack Obama and the secretary of state with John Kerry in the example below. You should use block alignments even if the two entities differ with respect to one word (e.g., Barack Obama vs. Barack H. Obama).

Finally, named entities can be recursive consisting of several named entities. In such cases, we recommend that you break the named entity into sub-parts each corresponding to one entity, instead of using a block alignment for the whole phrase. For example, if you were given the entites John Kerry of the United States and Mr Kerry, you should block align John Kerry with Mr Kerry, and leave of the United States unaligned.

Pronominal reference

When a pronoun is introduced in place of a name or a title, mark it is an alignment.

Determiners and prepositions

In cases where two noun phrases (NPs) or prepositional phrases (PPs) have the same head but different determiners (or prepositions) (e.g., a man vs. the man, a ceremony vs. their ceremony), the determiners should be aligned as possible. When an NP is missing a determiner altogether, then the determiner should be left unaligned (e.g., frost vs. the frost).

Tenses

Use possible block alignments in cases where the same verb is attested with different tenses (e.g., shall be vs. had been). Use certain block alignments when the tense is the same but the aspect differs.

To-clauses

When a to-clause (e.g. to applaud) corresponds to a verb in future tense (e.g. will praise), to shoudl be aligned with the modal denoting the future tense (e.g. will). When a verb does not have a future tense, to should be left unaligned (e.g. to have read vs have read)

Verb complexes

Verbs accompanied by modals and auxiliaries (e.g., will sleep, might have done, could be eating) should be considered one syntactic and semantic unit. Use certain many-to-one alignments in cases where such verb complexes in one sentence correspond to a single word in the other sentence (e.g., was developed ↔ developed, will sleep ↔ sleep).

Frames

In cases where the same verb is attested with differing frames (e.g. give to me vs give me) you should leave any extra material introduced by the different frame unaligned (e.g. give ↔ give, me ↔ me, to is left unaligned).

Use many-to-one alignment when a verb with a prepositional complement paraphrases a verb that takes a noun phrase complement, (e.g. spoke of ↔ mentioned).

Phrasal Verbs

Use a one-to-many alignment, in cases where a phrasal verb paraphrases a non-phrasal verb (e.g., take up your father vs. bring your father). In general phrasal verbs often take their meaning from the accompanying particle and should be considered as belonging to the verb. This can be a discontinuous alignment (e.g. give ... name ↔ call in the first example of this document).

Possessives

You should align the ’s maker with the word of and any article (e.g. a, the, this, etc.) that is also introduced.

Repetition

In cases where the same entity is represented in a sentence multiple times, introduce multiple alignments. The same holds not only for proper names (e.g., the UN ) but also for pronouns, verbs, and common nouns.

Rules of Thumb

While annotating keep in mind the following rules of thumb:

  1. Try to annotate as much as possible.
  2. Use block alignments when you are uncertain about which alignment to use.
  3. Block align only named entities differing in surface form.
